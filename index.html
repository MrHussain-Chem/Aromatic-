AROMATIC CHEMISTRY - AQA A-LEVEL CHEMISTRY REVISION

Aromatic compound (Arene): Contains benzene ring(s) with delocalised π electrons
Electrophile: Electron pair acceptor (positive ion or δ+ end of polar molecule)
Electrophilic substitution: H atom on benzene replaced by electrophile

1. BENZENE STRUCTURE - EVIDENCE AGAINST KEKULÉ

Kekulé Structure (Wrong!)
Alternating C=C and C-C bonds
Formula: C₆H₆
→
Actual Structure
All C-C bonds equal length (0.139 nm)
Delocalised π system

Key Evidence Against Kekulé

Test Expected (if Kekulé) Actual Result Explanation
Bromine water Decolourises (like alkenes) NO REACTION No localised C=C bonds
Bond lengths Alternating short/long All equal (0.139 nm) Intermediate between C-C and C=C
Hydrogenation ΔH -360 kJ mol⁻¹ -208 kJ mol⁻¹ More stable by 152 kJ mol⁻¹

The 152 kJ mol⁻¹ difference is the DELOCALISATION ENERGY - memorise this number! It shows benzene is more stable than expected due to electron delocalisation.

True Benzene Structure

  • Planar molecule - all atoms in same plane
  • 120° bond angles throughout
  • Each C is sp² hybridised with one p orbital
  • p orbitals overlap to form π system above AND below ring
  • 6 electrons delocalised in this π system

Comparing Reactivity: Cyclohexene vs Benzene

Property Cyclohexene Benzene
Electron system Localised π bond Delocalised π system
With Br₂(aq) Decolourises immediately No reaction
Type of reaction Addition Substitution (needs catalyst)
Why? High electron density at C=C attracts electrophiles Delocalised electrons less available

Why Methylbenzene is More Reactive than Benzene

Methyl group is electron-releasing (positive inductive effect)

  • Increases electron density in ring
  • Makes ring more attractive to electrophiles
  • Activates 2 and 4 positions (ortho/para directing)
  • Result: Methylbenzene reacts faster than benzene

2. WHY SUBSTITUTION NOT ADDITION?

Benzene undergoes SUBSTITUTION to preserve the stable delocalised π system

Addition would destroy aromaticity (lose 152 kJ mol⁻¹ stability)

3. ELECTROPHILIC SUBSTITUTION - GENERAL MECHANISM

ALL electrophilic substitution follows the SAME pattern - only the electrophile changes!

Three Steps (ALWAYS):

  1. Generate electrophile (usually needs catalyst)
  2. Attack by benzene - forms unstable intermediate
  3. Loss of H⁺ - restores aromaticity

4. NITRATION OF BENZENE

Reagents Conc. HNO₃ + Conc. H₂SO₄ (catalyst)
Conditions 50°C (higher temp gives dinitro products)
Electrophile NO₂⁺ (nitronium ion)
Product Nitrobenzene + H₂O

Mechanism Steps:

1. Generate NO₂⁺: HNO₃ + H₂SO₄ → NO₂⁺ + HSO₄⁻ + H₂O

2. Attack: Benzene attacks NO₂⁺ (arrow from ring to N)

3. Deprotonation: HSO₄⁻ removes H⁺, reforming H₂SO₄

Common mistake: Writing H⁺ leaves on its own. It MUST be removed by HSO₄⁻ to regenerate the catalyst!

Uses of Aromatic Nitro Compounds

  • Explosives: TNT (2,4,6-trinitrotoluene)
  • Dyes: Reduced to make aromatic amines for azo dyes
  • Pharmaceuticals: Starting materials for many drugs

Reduction of Nitrobenzene

Nitrobenzene → Phenylamine (aniline)

  • Reagents: Tin (Sn) + conc. HCl
  • Conditions: Heat under reflux
  • Equation: C₆H₅NO₂ + 6[H] → C₆H₅NH₂ + 2H₂O
  • Importance: Phenylamine used to make dyes and pharmaceuticals

5. FRIEDEL-CRAFTS ACYLATION

Reagents Acyl chloride (RCOCl) + AlCl₃ catalyst
Conditions Heat under reflux (anhydrous - NO water!)
Electrophile RCO⁺ (acylium ion)
Product Phenyl ketone + HCl

Mechanism Steps:

1. Generate RCO⁺: RCOCl + AlCl₃ → RCO⁺ + AlCl₄⁻

2. Attack: Benzene attacks RCO⁺

3. Deprotonation: AlCl₄⁻ removes H⁺ → HCl + AlCl₃

AlCl₃ is REGENERATED - it's a catalyst! Students often forget to show this in the final step.

6. FRIEDEL-CRAFTS ALKYLATION

Similar to acylation but:

  • Uses alkyl halide (e.g., CH₃Cl) instead of acyl chloride
  • Electrophile is R⁺ (carbocation)
  • WARNING: Multiple substitutions can occur!

7. COMPARISON OF REACTIONS

Reaction Electrophile How Generated Catalyst Returns?
Nitration NO₂⁺ HNO₃ + H₂SO₄ Yes (H₂SO₄)
FC Acylation RCO⁺ RCOCl + AlCl₃ Yes (AlCl₃)
FC Alkylation R⁺ RCl + AlCl₃ Yes (AlCl₃)
Halogenation Br⁺/Cl⁺ Br₂ + AlBr₃ Yes (AlBr₃)

8. ELECTROPHILE GENERATION - EXAM FOCUS

You MUST be able to write equations for electrophile generation!

Key Equations to Memorise:

  • Nitration: HNO₃ + 2H₂SO₄ → NO₂⁺ + H₃O⁺ + 2HSO₄⁻
  • FC Acylation: CH₃COCl + AlCl₃ → CH₃CO⁺ + AlCl₄⁻
  • FC Alkylation: CH₃Cl + AlCl₃ → CH₃⁺ + AlCl₄⁻
  • Bromination: Br₂ + AlBr₃ → Br⁺ + AlBr₄⁻

9. COMMON EXAM MISTAKES TO AVOID

Top 5 Mark-Losing Errors:

  1. Drawing mechanisms: Arrows must start from ring/bond, NOT from space
  2. Catalyst regeneration: MUST show H₂SO₄/AlCl₃ reformed
  3. Temperature for nitration: 50°C (NOT room temp or higher)
  4. Delocalisation energy: It's 152 kJ mol⁻¹ (memorise!)
  5. Intermediate charge: The + goes INSIDE the ring, not on a C

10. QUICK MECHANISM CHECKLIST

Before Drawing ANY Mechanism:

  • ☐ Step 1: Show electrophile generation equation
  • ☐ Step 2: Arrow FROM π electrons TO electrophile
  • ☐ Step 3: Draw intermediate with + INSIDE ring
  • ☐ Step 4: Arrow FROM C-H bond TO ring
  • ☐ Step 5: Show H⁺ removed by base (regenerates catalyst)
  • ☐ Check all formal charges shown
  • ☐ Check catalyst is regenerated

Common 4-6 Mark Question:

Q: Explain why benzene undergoes substitution rather than addition reactions.

Model Answer:

  • Benzene has delocalised π electrons (1 mark)
  • This makes it 152 kJ mol⁻¹ more stable than expected (1 mark)
  • Addition would break the π system and lose this stability (1 mark)
  • Substitution preserves the aromatic system (1 mark)

Final Tip: In mechanism questions, marks are given for:
1. Correct arrows (from electrons, not to them)
2. All intermediates shown
3. Correct charges
4. Catalyst regeneration
Practice drawing these until they're automatic!
